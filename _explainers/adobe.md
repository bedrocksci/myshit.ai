title: "Adobe: terms of service, changed overnight"
summary: "In 2024 Adobe updated a sentence in its license. Millions of designers woke up to discover the cloud knew more about their work than they did."
classification: public
date: 2026-04-24
---

In June 2024, Adobe pushed a revision to its terms of service. Designers, filmmakers, and agencies who opened Photoshop, Premiere, or Acrobat that week were greeted with an acceptance dialog. Buried in the new language was a phrase granting Adobe broad-looking rights to access and potentially use content that users stored in the cloud — including material that had been synced from years of prior work.

People read it as: Adobe is going to train AI on my confidential projects.

Adobe said that was not the intent. The language, they argued, was operational — needed for content moderation, anti-fraud, core product functions. That may have been true. But the fact that the language could be read the aggressive way was the point.

The reaction was immediate. Freelancers posted resignation threads. Studios with unreleased footage publicly weighed alternatives. Some enterprises with compliance requirements were legally required to pause use. Under pressure, Adobe walked the language back and published revised terms that narrowed the claim.

The backlash was the story. But the deeper story is the one nobody had to argue about: the work was sitting on Adobe's servers the whole time. The only thing that changed was the sentence describing what Adobe could do with it. A cloud-hosted creative subscription, by its nature, gives the vendor the ability to change terms unilaterally at any time, and users discover the new terms at login.

This keeps happening. Unity's retroactive per-install fees in 2023 blindsided game developers who had shipped years earlier under different terms. Reddit's 2023 API pricing change killed the third-party apps and mod tools that had been built on a decade of open access. Every one of these events followed the same arc: platform grows by offering a permissive deal, platform adjusts terms once the users are locked in.

AI tools are at the early, permissive phase of that arc. The next phase is the one you have seen before.

Files on your own computer don't get re-termed overnight. That's the only version of this story where you stay in charge.

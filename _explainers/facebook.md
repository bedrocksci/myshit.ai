Every Facebook user over thirty remembers the early days. Adding friends one by one. Tagging photos. Joining groups. Writing about the holiday, the band, the side project. Uploading the picture of yourself, piece by piece, because the site asked you to.

Nobody called it work. It felt like keeping in touch.

The deal, implicit: you built the picture; the site used it to connect you to people. For a while that was true.

Then the deal quietly changed. Organic reach — the thing that actually delivered your posts to people who had asked to see them — was throttled back. Businesses and creators who had built audiences on the platform found out that reaching those audiences now cost money. The inventory you had built with your own attention was rented back to you.

Then came Cambridge Analytica: in 2018, the personal data of roughly 87 million Facebook users was pulled via a third-party app and used for political ad targeting. Years of friend graphs, likes, relationships — the texture of normal life — compiled into psychographic profiles.

Then the ads machine. A decade of behavioral inference built on detailed, structured maps of who people are, what they want, what they'll buy.

None of this was the reason you joined. But it was the thing the picture was ultimately used for, and you didn't get to weigh in.

The hardest part: the work doesn't come back. Deleting your account doesn't undo a decade of uploading yourself into their servers. The picture stayed; you left empty-handed.

That's the pattern to hold in mind when an AI tool offers to "remember" you. The effort of teaching a machine who you are is the same kind of effort as adding friends to Facebook — quiet, unpaid, accumulating. If the picture lives on the tool's servers, the story ends the same way.

The alternative is simple: keep the picture somewhere you actually control.

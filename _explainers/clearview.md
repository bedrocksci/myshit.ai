In January 2020, a *New York Times* reporter named Kashmir Hill published a story about a small company almost nobody had heard of. The company had quietly scraped more than three billion photos from Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, YouTube, Venmo, and the open web — and built them into a face-search engine. Upload a photo of a stranger, and the system tries to return their name and everywhere else their face appears online.

The customers were police departments, initially. Then retailers. Then private investigators. Then, according to later reporting, a growing list of clients the company has been reluctant to name.

Facebook, Google, Twitter, YouTube, LinkedIn, and Venmo all sent cease-and-desist letters demanding Clearview stop scraping and delete what it had. The letters did not pull the images back. The photos had already been scraped, hashed, and indexed. You cannot un-scrape a database, only promise not to scrape the next one.

Regulators responded. The UK fined the company. France fined it. Italy fined it. Australia ordered it to delete Australian residents' data. A 2022 settlement in Illinois (under the Biometric Information Privacy Act) barred Clearview from selling the face-search product to most private entities within that state. The company has continued to operate, pay fines it can afford, and expand its contracts elsewhere.

If you uploaded a photo of yourself to any public social feed in the last fifteen years, your face is almost certainly in this database, or one like it. You didn't consent. You were never asked. The data went out a long time ago, and there is no mechanism for getting it back.

This is the hardest version of the pattern we keep seeing: content that you put out for one reason — to share with friends, to post for a job, to memorialize a moment — becomes, years later, the training data for a product that surveils you. Once it is out, you do not decide what happens next. Breaches, pivots, bankruptcies, resales, scrapers: every one of them is a way the picture escapes your control after you thought the transaction was complete.

Keep what's still yours, on your own computer. The rest of the internet will already do what it does.

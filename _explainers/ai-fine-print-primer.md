Every AI assistant released in the last year has a memory feature. They all say roughly the same thing in marketing copy — your assistant will remember you, learn your preferences, stop asking the same questions every session. This is genuinely useful. It is the single biggest thing that separates an AI tool that feels generic from one that feels like it knows you.

It is also the thing that turns a chatty utility into a long-term relationship with a company whose incentives you do not control.

Here is what the fine print of these features typically says, plain-language:

- The record of things you've told the assistant lives on the company's servers.
- The company may use that record to improve the service — which, in practice, can mean training future models, refining retrieval systems, and informing product decisions.
- The company may share pieces of it with partners they choose — infrastructure providers, analytics vendors, sometimes advertisers.
- The company may change the policy covering all of this at any time. You will usually be notified the next time you log in.
- If you want to leave, you can export a file. The picture the company built about you — the thing that made the tool useful for you — does not travel as cleanly as the raw text, and the copies already distributed don't come back.

For most people, for most conversations, none of this feels urgent. A chat about a coding problem is not a secret.

But the picture is not just the conversation. Over months and years of use, the memory becomes a high-resolution map of how you work, what projects you are on, who the people in your life are, the language you use to describe your own thinking, the decisions you have made and why. That level of depth is what makes the assistant useful. It is also the thing that sits on someone else's servers under terms they can adjust.

The way out is not to stop using AI. Frontier models are extraordinary, and the productivity curve is real. The way out is to keep the picture of yourself in a place you control — a folder of files, on your own computer, readable by you, sharable with whatever tool you choose, on terms you set.

What you build about yourself is work. It deserves to be owned.

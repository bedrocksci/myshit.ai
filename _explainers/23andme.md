The pitch was modest. Ninety-nine dollars, a tube of spit, and a week later you'd know where your grandmother's family really came from. Millions of people took the deal. Curiosity about ancestry is a small thing. Your genome is a large one.

The business people signed up for was ancestry. The business that was actually being run was data. 23andMe cut pharmaceutical research deals — most famously a large one with GlaxoSmithKline in 2018 — that used aggregated genetic data to hunt for drug targets. This was not hidden. It was also not why people ordered the kit.

In 2023, the company disclosed a breach. Attackers pulled data on roughly seven million users through a mix of credential stuffing and relative-matching features. Ethnicity, family connections, health risk markers. Some of it ended up for sale on forums. The class-action settlement came later.

In 2025, 23andMe filed for bankruptcy. The database of roughly fifteen million genomes became an asset on the balance sheet. Regulators and state attorneys general scrambled to figure out who could buy it, under what consent, and what the buyer would be allowed to do with it. That's not a question users ever expected to be part of.

The consent form people signed in 2012 did not anticipate a 2025 bankruptcy auction. It could not have. Consent at the time of sign-up is always consent to current arrangements — not to the arrangements that will exist once the business pivots, the executive team turns over, the company is sold.

Genetic data is the ultimate version of the pattern: something you cannot take back. You gave it for one reason. It can be used, quietly, for others. You do not decide which, and by the time you find out, the decisions are made.

AI memory is a softer version of the same shape. Less physically permanent, but the same logic: once the picture of you lives on someone else's servers, you are not the person who decides what happens next.
